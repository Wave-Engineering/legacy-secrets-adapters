The bootstrap problem

A materializer (shim, sidecar, agent) needs a credential to authenticate to its key source — say an OpenBao token. But where does that token live?

  • In a file? Then we're back to the "plaintext on disk" problem the delivery pattern solved.
  • In another vault? Who authenticates to that vault?
  • Hardcoded? Leaked with the image.

It's turtles all the way down — unless you terminate the regress at something that isn't a stored secret. Hardware is one such terminus.

Why a TPM ends the regress

Sealed blob (on disk)

ciphertextbound to PCRs Opaque. Useless without the TPM that sealed it, in the boot state it was sealed under.

Unsealed credential (in RAM)

momentarytmpfs Appears only at service start, lives only in memory, disappears on stop/reboot.

How it works

  At deploy time (seal ceremony):
    plaintext token ──> systemd-creds encrypt --with-key=tpm2 ──> sealed-blob.cred (on disk)

  At service start (automatic):
    systemd LoadCredentialEncrypted=bao-token:sealed-blob.cred
      → TPM2 unseal (PCR policy check)
      → plaintext appears in $CREDENTIALS_DIRECTORY/bao-token (tmpfs, mode 0400)

  Materializer reads $CREDENTIALS_DIRECTORY/bao-token → authenticates to OpenBao → done.

Composition with Cone of Silence

This pattern is a bootstrap pattern — it answers "how does the materializer authenticate without a stored secret?" The delivery side (how the secret reaches the unchanged legacy app) is handled by the Cone of Silence or a similar delivery pattern. They compose naturally:

  TPM unseal → materializer authenticates → fetches app secret → Cone delivers to app (RAM-only)
  [bootstrap]                                        [delivery]

Neither pattern alone solves the full problem. Together they cover both ends: the materializer's own credential (bootstrap) and the app's credential (delivery).

Tradeoffs — be honest

Hardware-bound

The credential cannot be migrated to another machine without re-sealing. This is the security property (stolen blob is useless) but also the operational cost (hardware replacement requires a reseal ceremony).

PCR brittleness

Any change to the measurements the credential was sealed against (firmware update, kernel upgrade, bootloader change) breaks unseal. The operational answer is a reseal ceremony: after a known-good update, re-seal the credential to the new measurements. Automating this ceremony is the main operational investment.

systemd coupling

This pattern uses systemd-creds and LoadCredentialEncrypted= — which means systemd ≥ 250 on a Linux host. Not portable to containers without a TPM passthrough, not portable to non-systemd inits. The alternative (direct tpm2-tools) is more portable but loses the ergonomics.

Still trusts the key source

The TPM authenticates the materializer to the key source, but you still trust the key source (OpenBao) to issue valid credentials and not be compromised. The bootstrap regress ends at silicon, but trust extends to the vault.

Delivery pattern — out of scope

This demo anchors the bootstrap secret (the materializer's OpenBao token). What consumes the anchored credential — a delivery pattern that materializes the secret to an unchanged app — is the orthogonal delivery-pattern family. See decision-guide.md.
